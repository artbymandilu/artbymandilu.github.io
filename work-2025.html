Gentle Breakage: 1/8 Model

This project addresses a common tension in urban nature spaces: while people go to parks and trails, they often remain confined to controlled paths, sidewalks, and designated areas, missing the opportunity to truly engage with the natural environment. My design creates a transitional experience that encourages exploration beyond these boundaries.

Located along the Three Rivers Heritage Trail near the Allegheny River in Pittsburgh, the project begins with a wooden structure that interrupts the rigid order of the sidewalk. Its geometric modules form an almost aggressive, wave-like shape that blocks the conventional path, guiding walkers inward while obscuring the exit. This moment of uncertainty compels people to actively engage with the structure and consider the space beyond the sidewalk.

Once inside, visitors encounter a concrete lattice that contrasts with the wooden entry. Its organic, fluid form has no prescribed paths, allowing for freedom of movement and experimentation. The lattice guides people past a brick wall and into the overgrown woods, a space that embodies risk, discovery, and a more authentic encounter with nature.

By creating this sequence from the controlled, predictable sidewalk to the open, explorative woods, the structure challenges passive park use. It invites visitors to leave behind habitual routines, interact with the space in new ways, and immerse themselves in the richness and unpredictability of the natural world. This project is not just about architecture or design; it is about creating a bridge between human order and the vitality of untamed nature, transforming how people experience the parks they visit.

Cloud and Moon Nightlight

I created this night light as a birthday gift for my younger brother. The arched poster board imitates the form of cumulus clouds, while the spherical container (previously packaging for a toy) appears as a moon following the cloud. I created several study models of the clouds (one of which is in the bottom right corner) before creating the final model with LED lights. This model fosters both stunning light and interwoven shadows, creating a simultaneous sense of both comfort and wonder.

Overlap

I created this piece at the CMU Pre-College Architecture Program. It was derived from a pattern by Esmeralda “Es” Devlin, shown on the left. To maintain a consistent language with Devlin’s pattern, I incorporated similar spiraling, geometric shapes, and overlapping colors. Just as the pattern’s texture remains consistent, I used consistent spiraling shapes and materials throughout. However, I used many differing colors, which overlap differently depending on one's viewpoint, to imitate the discontinuities in color across the pattern’s geometric shapes.

Dappled

I created this wearable while attending the Carnegie Mellon University Pre-college program. It incorporates elements from serial sections of Overlap. The 3D model that this wearable was partially derived from inspired me to explore further the materiality of light in how it spills and overlaps. The triangular and translucent colored plastics dapple colored light onto surrounding surfaces when near a light source. The elbow and wrist joints allow for complete mobility, giving the user control over how the dapples of light move and interact.

Dui Le

One of my favorite TED Talks is Canwen Xu’s, “I Am Not Your Asian Stereotype,” in which she discusses the gray space between familiarity and alienation that Asian Americans are often grouped into. This multi-media portrait explores the ambiguity of living between two cultures, featuring an Asian-American reaching through pages of Chinese and English newspapers, trying to find her way. The monochromatic colorscape creates a literal “gray space.” I used multiple layers of newspaper, holding their shape with hairspray, to create this effect.

Pittsburgh in the Summer

I made a pencil sketch of my view from a bus stop about 15 minutes southwest of Carnegie Mellon University while waiting there for a bus. I went over the sketch with ink following the bus ride, cleaned it up with a ruler, then improvised a few smaller nuances in the scenery that my sketch didn’t quite capture before erasing the pencil underneath it.

Reverie

In this work, I wanted to capture a physical embodiment of the mental sensations experienced when daydreaming. Daydreaming exists in a fragile balance: an escape into wandering thoughts while remaining grounded in the present moment. This relationship is expressed through several visual elements. The soft brushstrokes evoke the hazy, ephemeral quality of drifting into thought, while the subject’s rigid posture and focused gaze anchor the composition in reality.

Self Portrait

Many of my earliest childhood memories survive only as scattered fragments—sporadic moments, hazy images, and non-linear expressions suspended in time. I think it is strange that some of the most foundational facets of my identity can be so deeply eroded by passing time. This piece translates that experience into a physical form, using a structure that is deliberately irregular and unpredictable. Wooden dowels intersect and overlap in shifting arrangements, creating a sense of unpredictability. Many of these structures frame childhood photographs that I printed onto translucent vellum paper. Their delicate opacity allows the wooden framework to dominate visually. In this way, the dowels act as distortions and gaps in memory, reshaping and obscuring the images.

You Are a Mosaic of Everyone You’ve Ever Loved

This piece celebrates the influence of my closest friendships and the ways they continually shape who I am. I painted the facial features of my friends onto canvases, each capturing a fragment of their presence. Using steel wire, I constructed the outline of a head to function as an abstract framework that unites these separate portraits. By attaching the canvases to adhesive tack and threading the wire through them, I created a figure in which each friend’s traits merge into a single form. Just as these varied features come together to create one face, the many qualities of my friends intertwine to form the person I have become.

Study of Connections

This sculpture began as a material study in hand woodworking, focused on joints as both a technical and conceptual problem. Working entirely by hand, I explored how separate wooden elements can be connected, supported, and held together without fully disappearing into a seamless whole.

Spatial Study of a Deliverable

This series presents a hand-drawn, multi-angle axonometric study of a single deliverable (at left). Each drawing records a rotated view of the object, using line weight and repetition to articulate structure, density, and spatial hierarchy. Working by hand allows for small inconsistencies and moments of pressure to remain visible, foregrounding the act of drawing as a form of thinking.

First Cast: Raku-Inspired Concrete

This sculpture marks my first exploration of working with concrete, a material that, much like raku pottery, embraces unpredictability. In raku, pots often explode in the kiln, and success is never guaranteed; the process itself is as revealing as the outcome. Inspired by this, my piece captures a moment of transformation mid-explosion and is full of energy. The work reflects the beauty of imperfection and the tension between control and chance that emerges when materials act on their own.

Paper Kinetics

This sculpture explores the relationship between a material's uniformity and visual variety. Using only bond paper, I experimented with creating multiple textures and the suggestion of motion without any actual movement. The work reflects how a single, simple element can convey complexity, energy, and rhythm, inviting viewers to see possibility and dynamism in stillness.
